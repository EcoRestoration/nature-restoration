## 1. Peatlands, peat, fens, bogs, mires and wetlands: what's in a name?

In Joosten et al. (2017a), a wetland is defined as "an area that is inundated or 
saturated by water to the extent that its vegetation is dominated by plants that 
are adapted to life in anoxic soil conditions". The Convention of Wetlands defines 
wetlands as "areas of marsh, fen, peatland or water, whether natural or artificial, 
permanent or temporary, with water that is static or flowing, fresh, brackish or 
salt, including areas of marine water the depth of which at low tide does not 
exceed six metres".

The wetland definitions emphasize three important attributes:

- **Hydrology**: the degree of inundation and soil saturation
- **Vegetation**: plants adapted to a soil that is occasionally anoxic (oxygen 
  deficient) due to water saturation, often with the development of organic surface 
  horizons. When these conditions occur, dead plant material starts to accumulate 
  faster than it decomposes, thereby creating a layer of organic-rich material, 
  also known as peat.

Peat is described as sedentarily accumulated material consisting of at least 30% 
(dry mass) of dead organic material (Joosten et al., 2017a). Varying by country 
and scientific discipline, peatlands have been defined as having a minimum 
thickness between 20 and 70 cm. A commonly used minimum peat depth in Europe is 
30 cm, but this criterion excludes many (sub)arctic and (sub)alpine areas 
(Kivinen, 1980; Joosten et al., 2017). Within the World Reference Base (WRB), 
peat soils are considered Histosols (*from Gr. histos, tissue; connotative of 
soils rich in fresh or partly decomposed organic matter*). Although different 
terms have been used over the years, Histosols have always been characterized by 
organic soil material, its saturated conditions, its degree of decomposition, the 
depth of its occurrence and its thickness (Nachtergaele & Deckers, 2024).

A peatland is an area with a naturally accumulated layer of peat in the soil. 
A peatland that actively forms peat is considered a mire. A wetland with 
vegetation that may form peat is considered a swob.

Peat can be classified according to the material it is composed of:

- **Sphagnum peat** is primarily composed of Sphagnum mosses and is highly 
  absorbent. It is mostly found in nutrient-poor and acidic conditions, for 
  instance in bogs.
- **Sedge peat** is dominated by sedges and is often found in fens.
- **Reed peat** is formed in reedbeds, often in eutrophic swamp systems.
- **Forest peat** is formed within wet forest environments. The peat consists 
  of needles, leaves and woody debris mixed with other plant material. The 
  characteristics can vary depending on the type of forest (coniferous, 
  deciduous, tropical).

Peat formations can have successive layers of different peat materials and 
alternate with mineral layers.

Mires and peatlands are classified according to their properties, water table 
level and fluctuations, water origins, physicochemical properties of water and 
soil (pH, nutrient level, minerality, ...), vegetation structure & composition 
and peat stratigraphy. Some of the key classification components and their
